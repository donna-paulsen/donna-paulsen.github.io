CoproTech — Propiedad Horizontal SaaS

Kill the Excel spreadsheet that runs your building. Seriously.

🎯 Market Need

Colombia has 300,000+ copropiedades (condos, gated communities, office buildings) governed by Ley 675. Most are managed by a single overwhelmed administrador using WhatsApp groups, Excel spreadsheets, and paper receipts. The pain is universal and visceral:

  • Residents have zero visibility into where their cuotas de administración go
  • Collecting payments is a nightmare — 25-40% of buildings have chronic morosos (delinquent payers)
  • Assembly meetings are chaotic, quorum is never reached, and decisions go undocumented
  • Existing solutions (Kipo, Daytona, Properati) are either too enterprise or too basic — there's a massive mid-market gap

The TAM is real: if even 10% of copropiedades paid $15-30 USD/month for management software, that's a $5-10M/year market in Colombia alone.

💀 Execution Risk — Why This Will Fail

  • Administradores are tech-resistant boomers. You're selling software to people who think a PDF is cutting-edge
  • Sales cycle is painfully slow — requires assembly approval in most cases, and assemblies happen once a year
  • Switching cost is emotional, not technical. The current admin has "their system" (a notebook) and you're threatening their control
  • Payment collection integration with Colombian banks is a bureaucratic hellscape — PSE integration alone takes 2-4 months
  • Low willingness to pay: Colombian copropiedades are notoriously stingy. Getting $30/month feels like pulling teeth

  • Monetization: SaaS subscription per building + transaction fees on payment collection (0.5-1.5% of cuotas)

InformalOS — Gig Worker Financial Autopilot

55.4% of Colombia's workforce is informal. Zero of them have a financial operating system.

🎯 Market Need

Colombia has 12+ million informal workers (2024 OECD data: 55.4% of workforce). These are domiciliarios, vendedores ambulantes, peluqueros, mecánicos, freelance everything. Their financial reality:

  • PILA payments are a nightmare. The government portal (Mi Seguridad Social) is a UX disaster — independientes routinely overpay, underpay, or miss deadlines
  • No income tracking. Cash-based earnings mean zero visibility into actual monthly income, making tax compliance impossible
  • No insurance access. Informal workers are locked out of traditional insurance products — no ARL, no life insurance, no nothing
  • The 2025 pension reform (Ley de pilares) creates new compliance obligations for independientes earning ≥1 SMMLV, but nobody understands it

💀 Execution Risk — Why This Will Fail

  • Your users can't pay you. The painful irony: the people who need this most have the least willingness/ability to pay for software
  • Regulatory minefield. Anything touching PILA, pensions, or insurance requires licensing (UGPP oversight, Superfinanciera for insurance brokering)
  • Trust deficit. Informal workers distrust financial apps. They've been burned by "too good to be true" schemes. Getting them to input real income data? Good luck
  • Nequi and Daviplata already own the informal wallet. You're competing for the same phone screen real estate against banks with billions in marketing
  • Unit economics are brutal. Free users with low ARPU in a market that requires heavy hand-holding and support

Park this one unless you're ready to raise money and hire.

MediRuta — EPS Navigation & Advocacy Platform

The Colombian healthcare system is a labyrinth. This app is the Minotaur's GPS.

🎯 Market Need

Colombia's EPS system covers ~48 million people and is universally loathed. The pain points are systemic and deeply personal:

  • Specialist appointments take 30-90 days in the contributive regime. People literally die waiting
  • The referral chain is a black box. Patients bounce between EPS, IPS, and specialists with zero tracking — papers get "lost," referrals expire, nobody knows what's happening
  • Tutelas (legal health actions) are the only recourse — Colombians file 240,000+ health-related tutelas per year. Most people don't know how to file one or that they can
  • Medication tracking is nonexistent. Patients manage complex drug regimens with zero digital support from their EPS
  • The 2025 healthcare reform is creating even more confusion as EPS restructure and some collapse entirely

💀 Execution Risk — Why This Will Fail

  • EPS APIs don't exist. You'll be scraping portals, reverse-engineering appointment systems, and dealing with 15+ different EPS platforms, each one more broken than the last
  • Health data regulation (Ley 1581/Habeas Data + MinSalud regulations) is strict. One breach and you're done
  • EPS actively resist transparency. Your app's success means exposing their failures — they will try to block you
  • Tutela automation is a legal gray zone. If you auto-generate legal documents, you might cross into unauthorized legal practice territory
  • Monetizing healthcare navigation feels predatory. Charging people who can't get a doctor's appointment? Optics are rough

  • B2C: Freemium model — free appointment tracking, paid tier ($2-5/month) for tutela assistance, priority alerts, and medication reminders
  • B2B pivot: Sell the platform to prepagadas and seguros complementarios as a retention/engagement tool. This is where the real money is
  • Referral fees: Partner with private clinics for overflow appointments — patients willing to pay out-of-pocket for faster care
  • Scale: 48M EPS users, even 0.01% conversion = 4,800 paying users. At $3/mo that's $14.4K MRR
The play: Build the free tracker first — appointment dates, referral status, medication reminders. Get organic growth through sheer utility. Then monetize with the B2B angle (sell to medicina prepagada companies). The B2C revenue alone can hit your target, but B2B is the real margin play. Technical challenge is high — you're essentially building healthcare middleware for a system that doesn't want to be middleware'd.

RuralExpress — Tier-3 City Delivery Network

500+ Colombian municipalities have zero delivery infrastructure. That's a feature, not a bug.

🎯 Market Need

Colombia has 1,122 municipalities. Rappi operates in ~15. iFood/Didi in fewer. That leaves 1,000+ towns with growing smartphone penetration and zero on-demand delivery. The last-mile market is growing at 10%+ CAGR:

  • E-commerce penetration is rising but rural fulfillment is stuck in the 2010s — Servientrega/Coordinadora dominate with 3-7 day delivery windows
  • Local commerce is trapped. The restaurant in Quibdó or Sincelejo can't compete with Bogotá cloud kitchens because there's no delivery infra
  • Pharmacy and essentials delivery is non-existent in most tier-3 cities — huge unmet need for elderly and disabled populations
  • The motorcycle economy is already there. Every small town has mototaxistas with smartphones and no formal employment — instant fleet

💀 Execution Risk — Why This Will Fail

  • You can't scale this remotely. Each municipality needs local ops: onboarding restaurants, recruiting riders, managing cash. It's an ops business disguised as tech
  • Order density is death. Tier-3 cities have 20K-100K people. Even with 5% adoption, you're looking at 20-50 orders/day. Unit economics barely work
  • Rappi could enter any of these markets overnight if they become attractive enough — and they will outspend you 1000:1
  • Cash dominance. In these markets, 60-80% of transactions are cash-based. You need cash-on-delivery infrastructure, which is a logistical nightmare
  • Security concerns. Some tier-3 areas have complex security dynamics. Your rider carrying cash + a smartphone is a target

You'd need local managers in each city at $300-500/month each. Not impossible at $5-10K/mo net, but it's tight and fragile. Better suited for someone with a co-founder in the region.

RemesaStack — Freelancer FX & Billing OS

200,000+ Colombian freelancers earn in USD and lose 3-8% converting it. Every. Single. Month.

🎯 Market Need

Colombia's remote work revolution is real. An estimated 200,000+ Colombians earn USD through platforms like Upwork, Toptal, Deel, and direct contracts. Their financial stack is broken:

  • FX spread robbery. Banks charge 3-5% on USD→COP conversion. Even Wise takes 1-2%. For someone earning $3K/month, that's $60-150 lost monthly
  • DIAN compliance is a maze. Freelancers must issue electronic invoices (factura electrónica), file IVA, report foreign income, and handle retención en la fuente — most just... don't
  • Double taxation risk. Without proper estructura tributaria, freelancers overpay or face DIAN audits. The 2023 tax reform made this worse
  • No single tool connects FX + invoicing + tax. They juggle Wise + a contadora + Excel + DIAN portal + their bank. It's 5 systems doing what 1 should
  • The "persona natural vs SAS" decision paralyzes people for months. Everyone needs guidance, nobody can get it without paying $200/hour to a tax attorney

💀 Execution Risk — Why This Will Fail

  • Regulatory complexity is extreme. Handling money movement (even as a referral layer) puts you in Superfinanciera's crosshairs. Full SEDPE or SEI licensing costs $100K+
  • Wise, Payoneer, and Remitly are entrenched. You can't beat them on FX rates. Your value-add has to be the Colombian layer on top
  • Tax advisory liability. If your app recommends a tax structure and the DIAN audits the user, you're potentially liable. Need very careful disclaimers
  • Market ceiling. 200K freelancers sounds big, but the addressable segment (earning enough to care about optimization) is maybe 50-80K
  • Contadoras are the competition. Every freelancer already has a contadora charging 150,000-300,000 COP/month. Your app needs to replace OR complement them

  • Core revenue: SaaS subscription — $10-25/month for automated DIAN invoicing, tax calendar, and income tracking
  • FX arbitrage layer: Partner with a licensed FX provider (not be one) and take a referral cut (0.1-0.3% of volume). Even modest volume generates thousands
  • Contadora marketplace: Connect users with vetted accountants and take a 15-20% referral fee on the first year
  • Tax filing upsell: Charge $30-50 for guided annual tax filing (declaración de renta) — 80K+ potential buyers once/year
  • COP reality: $15/month = ~57,000 COP. Compare that to a contadora at 200,000 COP. The value prop is obvious
The play: This is your best bet. You ARE the target user — a systems engineer earning USD in Colombia. Build what you need, then productize it. Start with the DIAN invoicing module (it's the sharpest pain point), add income tracking, then layer in FX comparison and tax guidance. You can have 100 paying users in 3 months by posting in a single Colombian freelancer Facebook group. The path to $10K/month is: 600 users × $17/month average. Very achievable in 6-9 months with solid execution.
